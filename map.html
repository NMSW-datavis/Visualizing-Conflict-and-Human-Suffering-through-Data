The Shape of Conflict

A data-driven investigation into how global instability is concentrating, shifting, and intensifying in 2024.

“To understand the true cost of instability, we must look beyond the borders and look at the trend lines.”

The world is not evenly at peace or at war. Conflict lives on a spectrum. While headlines focus on specific flashpoints, the underlying data reveals a complex architecture of risk.

By analyzing civilian fatality reports from 2024 and comparing them against a five-year baseline, we can deconstruct global violence into three distinct dimensions: Intensity, Momentum, and Concentration.

Our first view captures the absolute magnitude of loss. The map below highlights countries based on total reported civilian fatalities in 2024. Darker shades of red indicate the severe hotspots—Ukraine, Sudan, Gaza, and Myanmar—where the human toll has been highest.

However, a static map can be misleading.

Finally, we must recognize that violence rarely respects political treaties. It spills across frontiers, fueled by porous borders, shared insurgencies, and regional geopolitics.

By removing national boundaries and visualizing the data as a density field, we see the true "epicenters" of global suffering. Individual conflicts in the Sahel, the Levant, and Eastern Europe merge into massive, interconnected zones of instability.

These maps remind us that "hot spots" are not isolated islands. They are part of a shifting, breathing ecosystem of instability that requires regional, not just national, attention.
