The Architecture of Instability

How patterns of violence reveal the hidden geometry of political disorder.

“Instability is rarely random. Its structure, once mapped, is disquietingly consistent.”

In the study of modern conflict, geography still dominates the imagination. Wars are defined by their coordinates: Gaza, Donbas, Rakhine, Michoacán. Yet when violence is examined not by where it happens but by how it behaves, a different landscape appears—one shaped not by borders but by structure.

The network below maps this alternative world. Countries are pulled toward one another not by distance but by mathematical similarity: the number of armed groups, the volatility of fighting, and the degree to which civilians are targeted. These structural traits form distinct signatures, binding unlikely nations together.

Most countries cluster along familiar regional lines. Yet one pairing stands out. In the Anomaly view, Mexico and Nigeria emerge as structural twins: each beset by a dense constellation of competing non-state actors, each experiencing unpredictable spikes of civilian harm, and each locked in a prolonged struggle for territorial control. Geography separates them; their instability framework does not.

The Human Cost

Structural patterns tell us how conflicts behave. The Sankey diagram below shows what they produce. Fatalities flow from regional origins into the forms of violence that claim lives: battles, insurgencies, non-state clashes, and, most grimly, One-Sided Violence.

These are deaths in which civilians are killed without resistance—executions, massacres, and targeted killings. In several regions, this “shadow war” exceeds the casualties of open battle.

Conflict, viewed structurally, is less chaotic than it appears. It moves in predictable patterns, even as its human toll remains immeasurable. Behind each line, node, and flow are lives reshaped or lost.
