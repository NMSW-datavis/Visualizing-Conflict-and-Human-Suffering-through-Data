The Distribution of Suffering

What a conflict 'hot spot' really looks like, told through data.

← Back to Home
“To understand the distribution of suffering, we must first see its contours”

The world is not uniformly at peace or at war. Instead, conflict exists on a spectrum. Some nations experience low-level, simmering tension, while others are designated as 'Extreme' hot spots.

But what does "Extreme" risk truly mean? Is it a constant state of war, or something else? By combining a present-day snapshot with historical data, we can move from a simple list of 'at-risk' countries to a deep understanding of the human experience within them.

Part 1: The Global Landscape of Conflict

Our analysis begins with a global snapshot of conflict in 2024. The ACLED Conflict Index measures risk based on deadliness, danger, and fragmentation, assigning each country a Total Score.

This histogram shows the distribution of those scores. It is heavily skewed to the left, revealing a stark reality: while most of the world experiences low conflict (the large cluster), a small number of countries (the long tail on the right) face 'High' and 'Extreme' levels of danger. These are the world's most acute hot spots.

Part 2: The Human Cost of Volatility

Now, we zoom in on the "Extreme" hot spots. To understand the human cost, we turn to historical data, plotting the distribution of reported civilian fatalities for each of the top 7 most-affected nations.

This box plot reveals that 'Extreme' conflict is not one-size-fits-all. The median line (inside the box) shows the 'typical' number of fatalities in a year. The box itself shows the "normal" range of volatility, while the long whiskers show the 'worst-case' years.

Notice how some countries have a high median, showing a consistently high death toll. Others have a lower median but an incredibly tall box and long whiskers. This tells a different story: one of extreme volatility, where life is shattered by catastrophic, outlier years.

Part 3: The Shape of Danger

The violin plot gives us the richest detail, showing the shape of the data. A wide belly means many years had that number of fatalities. A thin, long tail shows rare, extreme events.

Here, we can see the true 'texture' of conflict. Some plots are wide at the bottom (near zero) but have a long, thin tail, confirming the risk of a catastrophic outlier year. Others may have two distinct 'bellies,' suggesting the country flips between 'low-level conflict' and 'all-out war,' with very few years in between.

“Data shows that for civilians in these regions, the threat isn't just the level of conflict, but its volatility and unpredictability.”

A 'hot spot' is not just a place with high numbers; it's a place where the shape of the data itself—the constant risk of a 'normal' year becoming a catastrophic one—defines daily life.
