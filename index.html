Visualizing Conflict and Human Suffering

Exploring the human stories behind data on global conflicts

by MNSW Mohammed Yassin, Nahid Davoudi, Sebah Tewodros Tesfatsion, Wassim Fatnassi

About This Project

“Behind every data point lies a human story — of loss, displacement, and resilience.”

This project seeks to illuminate the realities of global conflict and its humanitarian toll. Through careful data visualization, we aim to translate complex datasets into accessible narratives that foster empathy and understanding.

Our visualizations will draw from respected sources including ACLED and Our World in Data. We emphasize clarity, context, and the ethical presentation of human data.

Patterns in Human Displacement

Understanding how conflict reshapes populations across regions

This section delves deeper into the data — exploring how violence and instability lead to massive shifts in population, and how these numbers reflect broader humanitarian trends. Through different visual representations, we highlight both the scale and the distribution of suffering caused by ongoing conflicts.

Population Displacement by Region

This histogram (population pyramid) compares displaced male and female populations across major conflict zones, showing the demographic imbalance often caused by violence.

Civilian Fatalities in Russia and Ukraine (2017–2025)

Comparison of annual reported civilian fatalities in Russia and Ukraine
The side-by-side histograms show a striking contrast in the scale of civilian deaths between the two countries. In Ukraine©, civilian fatalities surge dramatically in 2022 — reaching nearly 6,000 deaths — coinciding with the full-scale invasion. Although the numbers decline slightly afterward, they remain alarmingly high, above 2,000 fatalities per year.

In Russia, the civilian toll is comparatively low throughout the same period, with minor increases in 2023–2025 peaking around 600 deaths.

Together, these histograms emphasize the asymmetric humanitarian impact of the conflict — where Ukrainian civilians have borne the overwhelming share of suffering and loss.

Comparative Severity Index

The boxplot summarizes the intensity of conflicts by region — showing median, quartiles, and extreme edvents. Each box represents a country group affected during the study period.
